Phishing & Scam Defence

Modern scams look real. Many are AI-generated, personalised and designed to trigger quick reactions. This guide helps you spot them before they cause harm.

Why Scams Work So Well

Scammers don’t guess — they use psychology. They want you to react fast, panic, click or share before thinking. Even confident adults fall for modern scams because they look professional and familiar.

What You Should Know:
  • Most scams begin through text, email or social messaging — not hacking.
  • Fake delivery updates, bank alerts and government messages are extremely convincing.
  • Scammers now use AI to write perfect English and mimic real companies.
  • Children are targeted through gaming chats and “free Robux / free skins” scams.
  • Scams succeed when the victim acts quickly, not when they are “stupid”.

How to Recognise a Scam Instantly

1. Unexpected Urgency

Anything that says “urgent”, “final notice”, “your account will be closed” is designed to trigger panic.

2. Requests for Personal Information

Banks, HMRC, the NHS and major companies will never ask you to confirm passwords or full details via text/email.

3. Strange or Shortened Links

Hover over the link or press and hold on mobile — if it looks unusual, don’t click.

4. Asking for Payment or Codes

Scammers request gift cards, bank transfers, PayPal “family & friends” or security codes.

5. Too Good To Be True Offers

Free Robux, free skins, free cryptocurrency, free credit — these are designed to steal accounts.

Protecting Your Household

1. Always Pause Before You Click

A 3-second pause prevents 99% of scam mistakes. Slow reactions save you.

2. Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

This blocks most unauthorised logins, even if your password leaks.

3. Don’t Respond to Unknown Messages

Never reply to suspicious texts — replying confirms your number is active.

4. Teach Children About “Fake Rewards”

Explain that scammers try to steal accounts by offering free items or pretending to be moderators.

5. Verify Everything on the Official Website

If a bank or service contacts you, ignore the message and log in manually through their website/app.
